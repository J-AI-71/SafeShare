Tracking parameters explained

No panic. No fog. Just the essentials.

Tracking parameters are additions in URLs, usually after ?. Examples: utm_source, fbclid, gclid. They can help with campaign analytics, but are often unnecessary when sharing.

What is it exactly?

Base URL

https://example.com/article

With parameters

https://example.com/article?utm_source=newsletter&utm_campaign=launch&fbclid=XYZ

The page can be identical — only the appended metadata differs.

What do UTM fields mean?

utm_source

Source, e.g. newsletter, linkedin, mastodon.

utm_medium

Channel type, e.g. email, social, cpc.

utm_campaign

Campaign name, e.g. spring_launch.

utm_term / utm_content

Optional: keywords/variants (A/B, ad variation).

Other common tracking IDs

fbclid

Click ID from Facebook/Meta context.

gclid

Google Ads click ID.

msclkid

Microsoft Ads click ID.

mc_eid / mc_cid

Newsletter/mailing IDs (e.g., Mailchimp).

Why remove them before sharing?

Fewer data traces

You pass along less extra metadata in chats, emails, and docs.

Cleaner links

Shorter, clearer, more professional-looking URLs.

Less breakage risk

Very long links are less likely to break in tools/editors.

Intentional sharing

You decide what metadata is included.

When not to remove blindly

Affiliate / referral

Keys like ref or tag may be intentional attribution.

Functional parameters

Some keys control language, filters, IDs, or search state.

Good practice: remove standard tracking keys, but review functional/attribution keys deliberately.
